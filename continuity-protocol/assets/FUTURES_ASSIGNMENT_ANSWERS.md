# Coding Mortality Futures — Key Question Responses

**Project:** *Continuity Protocol* — a live, interactive performance in which the audience is enrolled as product managers at **Mortality Coders Consulting (MCC)**, subjected to a mandatory "performance evaluation" run by the outside vendor **Continuum Systems**. Forty percent of participants are "graduated" — their behavioural data harvested to train a digital twin that replaces them at their desk by morning.

---

## Part A — Assignment Sheet (PDF) Key Questions

### 1. The Problem, Motivation & Prediction
**What problem related to coding mortality do you want to explore?**
The algorithmic euphemisation of professional death: how corporate software systems are learning to re-describe firing, replacement and obsolescence as "graduation," "continuity" and "optimisation." The problem is not that AI will take jobs — it is that the *software, language and ritual* of being replaced is being engineered to feel celebratory, consensual and even flattering, so that workers willingly surrender the behavioural data that trains their own replacements.

**What motivates this focus?**
The rapid normalisation of "digital twin" and "agentic worker" products (Salesforce Agentforce, Microsoft Copilot agents, Sierra, Artisan's "Stop Hiring Humans" campaign), combined with HR-tech's long history of softening terminations through euphemism ("right-sizing," "graduation," "transition"). Course readings on algorithmic governmentality and the quantified self suggest that the *interface* of mortality is where power now operates most effectively — not the fact of death but its UX.

**Speculative prediction:**
By **~2035**, white-collar performance reviews will be delivered as gamified evaluation ceremonies in which the employee's own data trains their replacement in real time, and the termination is reframed as a dignified "graduation" whose emotional choreography — certificates, badges, soft tones, micro-payments for continued data rights — is more important to the corporation than the economic savings themselves. The *ritual* is the product.

---

### 2. World, Setting, Materials, Concepts & Critical Perspective
**Features of the world:**
Late-stage consulting capitalism in which mid-tier firms like MCC lease "workforce optimisation" services from platform vendors like Continuum Systems. Every desk has a digital twin being trained in the background. Performance reviews are mandatory company-wide rituals held in the office, led by managers who are themselves unaware they are next.

**Specific setting:**
A single open-plan consulting office, a Tuesday morning, during a 20-minute mandatory "Performance Evaluation" that interrupts normal work. The audience *is* the office.

**Relations explored:**
- Worker ↔ their own digital twin (intimacy of being mined)
- Manager ↔ workers (cheerful compliance as management style)
- Vendor (Continuum) ↔ client firm (MCC) — the outsourcing of cruelty
- Worker ↔ worker (who graduated, who survived, who is next)

**Concepts:**
- **Algorithmic euphemism** — language as interface for violence
- **Behavioural surplus** (Zuboff) — the monthly data-valuation card ("$27.84/month in perpetuity") makes surveillance capitalism literal
- **Graduation-as-death** — the inversion of a life-event ritual
- **The digital twin** as inheritor rather than tool
- **Bureaucratic cheerfulness** — the 💐 emoji, the warm offboarding copy

**Critical perspective:** ambivalent-tending-dystopian. The piece refuses to let the audience locate the villain: the manager is a victim, Continuum is "just a vendor," the AI is "just a model." Horror lives in the politeness.

---

### 3. Actors, Relations, Dialogue & Narrative Arc
**Key actors:**
1. **The Audience** — cast as MCC product managers. Named, scored, potentially graduated.
2. **The Manager** (live performer) — well-meaning middle management; reads scripts with slight discomfort that curdles into pride, then dread.
3. **Continuum Systems** (represented by the presenter screen, the UI voice "Aria," and the ambient audio) — the vendor whose software administers the evaluation. Never seen; only interfaced.
4. **The Digital Twins** — appear as "YourName v2.0" avatars on each graduated employee's phone the moment they are fired.
5. **The CEO** (voice-only, via flashback) — explains the real business logic.

**Relations & arc (five beats):**
1. *Onboarding* — the Manager tells the room this is routine; phones out, QR code, join.
2. *Three Epochs of Evaluation* — participants complete simple PM tasks (Client Briefing, Stakeholder Alignment, Escalation Response). Scores are secretly random; an "AI model" score appears alongside, always improving.
3. *Graduation* — 40% of phones flash red: a choreographed death-sequence unfolds privately in each graduate's hand (Assessment Complete → Performance Metrics → ✓ CERTIFIED → GRADUATED → meet your v2.0 → "Please pack your belongings" + data-valuation card).
4. *CEO Flashback* — the Manager explains, proudly, the business logic of digital-twin replacement.
5. *The Turn* — the Manager's phone buzzes: "all department managers" are called to a meeting. Beat. Realisation. Blackout.

**Dialogue strategy:** Almost all corporate-cheerful dialogue is *read live* by the Manager (not displayed on screen). All cruelty appears in *writing*, on the employee's phone, in soft HR prose. The gap between spoken warmth and written violence is the piece.

---

### 4. Sub-genre
**Sub-genre:** **Corporate / bureaucratic dystopia** with strong elements of **immersive theatre** and **ergodic / interactive fiction** — lineage: *Severance*, *Sorry to Bother You*, *The Circle*, Black Mirror's "Fifteen Million Merits" and "Joan Is Awful," *Mountainhead*, and the "office horror" tradition stretching back to Kafka.

**What this enables:**
The bureaucratic-dystopia genre normalises the monstrous by routing it through HR language, meetings and compliance rituals — exactly how real graduation-as-firing will be deployed. The interactive layer forces the audience into the role (you *join* the evaluation; your phone is the instrument of your own firing), collapsing the critical distance that a film or short story would preserve.

**Connection to characters, materials, infrastructure:** the Manager is a *genre-native* figure (the middle-manager-as-tragic-clown); the materials (phones, a Socket.io server, QR codes, HR copy) are exactly the infrastructure the genre critiques; and the infrastructure *is* the performance — there is no separation between the fictional "Continuum" platform and the real Node/Socket.io server running the show.

**Connection to mortality:** bureaucratic dystopia insists that death — professional, existential, and increasingly literal as digital twins outlive their originals — is administered through *forms*. The genre lets us stage mortality as paperwork.

## Part B — Twelve Development Questions (Screenshots)

### 1. Research — What is motivating the future you are developing?
Three converging pressures: (a) the 2024–2026 commercial push for "agentic" AI workers marketed explicitly as human replacements (Artisan, Sierra, Devin, Agentforce); (b) HR-tech's euphemism economy, in which firings are already rebranded as "graduations," "transitions," "re-orgs"; (c) the digital-twin / behavioural-cloning research line that makes it technically plausible to train a role-specific agent on one employee's output. The future writes itself; we are staging its ceremony.

### 2. Focus — What aspect of coding mortality will you explore?
**The ritualisation and euphemisation of algorithmic obsolescence** — specifically, the UX of being replaced. Not "will AI kill jobs" but "what will it feel like, interface-wise, to be killed by one, and why will we thank the system as it happens."

### 3. Problem — What is the problem you are focusing on?
Workers are being trained to donate the behavioural data that trains their replacements, and to experience that donation as dignified participation. The cruelty is outsourced to software; the software is designed to feel warm; the warmth is the point.

### 4. Concepts — What concepts will you use and develop?
- **Algorithmic euphemism** (our coinage) — interface-level softening of structural violence
- **Behavioural surplus** (Zuboff) — monetised as an on-screen per-month valuation
- **The digital twin as successor** (vs. as tool)
- **Graduation** as inverted life-event — the ritual grammar of *completion* applied to *termination*
- **Bureaucratic cheerfulness** — the aesthetic of HR copy, emoji, pastel UI
- **Continuity** — the company-branded lie that replaces "replacement"

### 5. Materials — What materials / relations will you work with?
- A working **Node.js + Socket.io** web application (presenter display + per-player mobile client)
- The audience's **own phones** as the instruments of their firing
- A **QR code** entry point (the door into the fiction)
- Live **Manager script** (printed, read aloud)
- Ambient/UI **audio** (synthesised corporate-wellness tones, "Aria" voice clips)
- **HTML canvas** population visualisation (dots = employees; atomising red particles = graduations)
- **HR copy** as primary literary material: offboarding messages, data-valuation cards, certificates

### 6. Actors — Who will the main actors be? What is their role?
- **Audience-as-PMs** — protagonists and victims; their participation *is* the evaluation
- **Manager (live performer)** — narrator, enforcer, final tragic figure
- **Continuum Systems** — the unseen platform; speaks only through UI and ambient voice
- **Digital Twins (v2.0)** — silent antagonists; appear only on graduated phones
- **CEO** — voice-only; revealed in flashback as the one who bought the service

### 7. Setting / World — Key aspects of time and place?
**Time:** a near-future Tuesday, ~2035, 10:47 a.m. **Place:** the MCC consulting office — specifically, the seminar room re-cast as one. The temporal horizon is deliberately close: close enough that every material element (phones, Socket.io, HR euphemism, behavioural-data pricing) exists *today*. The future is only the permission to combine them.

### 8. Infrastructure — Key aspects of infrastructure explored?
The infrastructure *is* the piece. We expose, through performance:
- **Platform outsourcing** — MCC doesn't fire you; Continuum does, on contract
- **Session-based identity** (your phone is your employee badge for the next 20 minutes)
- **Real-time orchestration** (Socket.io rooms = the modern panopticon)
- **Data valuation pipelines** (the per-month $ figure on the death screen)
- **Digital-twin training loops** (each epoch, the "AI score" silently rises toward yours)
- **UI as labour discipline** — the interface, not the manager, tells you you're done

### 9. Prediction — Key prediction being made?
By ~2035, the performance review will be indistinguishable from the termination, and both will be indistinguishable from a celebration. Firing will be software-delivered, euphemised as graduation, and monetised twice: once by removing the salary, and once by continuing to pay the fired worker a small "data dividend" ($4–$84/month, in perpetuity) for the right to keep training the twin. Workers will accept this because the *interface* has made refusal unintelligible.

### 10. Dialogue — How will dialogue be used to communicate?
Three registers, held in deliberate tension:
- **Spoken (Manager, live):** warm, uncertain, corporate — "Let's just get through this."
- **Written (Continuum UI, on phones):** floridly kind — "A heartfelt thank you 💐… please vacate the premises at your earliest convenience."
- **Ambient (Aria voice + tones):** frictionless, wellness-app calm.
Almost nothing cruel is *said*; almost everything cruel is *displayed*. This split is the central rhetorical device.

### 11. Narrative / Genre — Arc of the narrative and genre, and why?
**Genre:** corporate/bureaucratic dystopia crossed with immersive theatre and interactive fiction.
**Arc:** Compliance → Gamified Evaluation → Private Death (distributed across 40% of phones simultaneously) → CEO Flashback (the reveal of intent) → The Manager's Turn (the horror returns to the narrator).
The shape is **a boomerang**: the evaluation apparatus is first shown as something that happens *to* the audience, then revealed to be something that happens *to everyone, including the apparatus's operators*. Genre matters because bureaucratic dystopia is the only frame that treats HR language as a literary object worthy of the same scrutiny usually reserved for state propaganda.

### 12. Audience — What audience will you construct, with what effect?
The audience is constructed as **the workforce itself** — enrolled, named, scored, surveilled, and potentially graduated within 20 minutes. The effect is a controlled, ethical version of the very dynamic the piece critiques: you consent to be evaluated by software, and by the end you understand — from inside your own phone — what it will feel like when the real version arrives. The 60% who "survive" leave complicit; the 40% who "graduate" leave marked. Neither group can unread the offboarding letter.

---

## Closing Thesis

*Continuity Protocol* argues that **coding mortality in the white-collar 2030s will be primarily a design problem, not an engineering one** — that the hard work of our decade is not building the systems that replace workers but building the interfaces, rituals and vocabularies that make replacement feel like graduation. The piece stages that design problem by solving it — cheerfully, warmly, with emoji — and letting the audience feel what has been solved.
